Elusive Hearing channel

This afternoon finally I have got enough time to dig a little bit into this topic.

Deafness could be categorized as syndromic (30%) and nonsyndromic (70%). for the nonsyndromic deafness, there are 4 types:

  • autosomal recessive, DFNB
  • autosomal dominant, DFNA
  • mitochondial,
  • X-linked, DFNX
  • Moddifier; DFNM

and severity

  • Mild:
    • for adults: between 26 and 40 dB HL
    • for children: between 20 and 40 dB HL
  • Moderate: between 41 and 54 dB HL
  • Moderately severe: between 55 and 70 dB HL
  • Severe: between 71 and 90 dB HL
  • Profound: 91 dB HL or greater
  • Totally Deaf: Have no hearing at all.

In omim.org, Hearing lost/deafness will show you a bunch of related gene, revealing many phenotypes of deafness. Among those phenotype, Autosomal recessive non-syndromic deafness is impressive because it is less likely because of the downstream neuronal transduction and more directly reflecting the defect of mechanotransduction in hair cells.

I found this great recent review summarising all genes associated with autosomal recessive non-syndromic deafness. Among the long list of genes, I am interested to these genes especially.

  • TMC1

    • Nature Genetics 2012 two papers

      • http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11850618
      • http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/11850623
      • Identified it’s related to hearing lost and generated mutant mice.

    • JCI 2011 paper

      • http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/22105175
      • found that Tmc1 -/- mice were deaf.

    • Nature 2013 paper

      • http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23364694
      • TMC1 encode sodium channel and sense salt in C.elegans.

    • Neuron 2013 paper

      • http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/23871232
      • these two are channel components responsible for mechanotransduction in hair cells

  • BRN-4

    • Science Paper 1999
      • http://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pubmed/10464101
      • severe ultrastructural alterations in cochlear spiral ligament
  • barttin: Chloride channel.

Many gap junctions/tight junctions proteins are involved in ARNHL, and these proteins are crucial to maintain K+ gradient and potential, thus I guess the unknown mechanosensitive channel shall be a potassium channel.

If the MET channel is really responsible for hearing. At least several prerequisite conditions shall apply:

  • Prelingual deafness.
  • Sensorineural hearing loss.
  • hereditary hearing loss in patients or laboratory animals.
  • membrane protein.

and most likely be:

  • potassium channel (because gap junctions and tight junctions malfunction will lead to hearing loss)
  • with large or/and multiple transmembrane domain (a channel protein)
  • unknown functions.

A nice online database listed many deafness and the potential underlying genes. I shall filter those potential MET genes but it’s a lot of work to do.
